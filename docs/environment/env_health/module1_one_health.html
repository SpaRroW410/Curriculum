This module introduces the foundational One Health concept, critical for understanding environmental health challenges, particularly water-related issues. The integration of human, animal, and environmental health is essential for addressing complex public health problems.

What is One Health?

Definition:

One Health is a collaborative, multisectoral, and transdisciplinary approach working at local, regional, national, and global levels with the goal of achieving optimal health outcomes.

Core Recognition:

The health of people is closely connected to the health of animals and our shared environment.

Source: U.S. Geological Survey, 2024

U.S. Government Definition established in 2017. The approach recognizes that approximately 60% of emerging infectious diseases in humans are zoonotic, originating from animals. Environmental factors like water quality directly impact both human and animal health.

The One Health Triad

Three Interconnected Pillars

1. Human Health - Disease surveillance and control - Public health infrastructure - Healthcare systems - Epidemiological monitoring

2. Animal Health - Veterinary services - Livestock management - Wildlife health monitoring - Zoonotic disease tracking

3. Environmental Health - Water quality and sanitation - Air pollution control - Soil health - Ecosystem biodiversity

Each pillar affects and is affected by the others. For example, antimicrobial use in livestock (Animal Health) can contaminate water sources (Environmental Health), leading to antimicrobial resistance affecting humans (Human Health).

Why One Health Matters for Water

The Water-Health Nexus

Water as a Critical Interface:

  • Pathogen Transmission: Waterborne zoonotic diseases (leptospirosis, schistosomiasis)
  • Chemical Contamination: Agricultural runoff (nitrates, pesticides) from animal farming
  • Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR): Antibiotics from animal waste entering water systems
  • Environmental Reservoirs: Water bodies as habitats for disease vectors (mosquitoes, snails)

One Health Application:

Water contamination requires integrated solutions addressing human practices (sanitation), animal management (waste disposal), and environmental protection (watershed management).

Example: Nitrate contamination in groundwater results from excessive use of animal manure and chemical fertilizers (agriculture/animal health), contaminates drinking water (environmental health), and causes methemoglobinemia in infants (human health). This exemplifies the One Health interconnection.

One Health Surveillance: Monitoring water sources for both human and animal pathogens provides early warning of disease outbreaks.

Leptospirosis outbreaks in India often follow monsoon flooding when rat urine contaminates floodwaters. One Health approach requires coordinated rodent control, sanitation improvement, and public health awareness.

Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) in Water

A Growing Crisis

The AMR-Water Connection:

  • Source: 75-90% of antibiotics administered to livestock are excreted unchanged
  • Pathway: Animal waste → manure → agricultural runoff → surface/groundwater
  • Impact: Resistant bacteria and resistance genes contaminate drinking water sources

Consequences:

  1. Human Health: Infections become harder to treat
  2. Animal Health: Reduced efficacy of veterinary antibiotics
  3. Environmental Health: Disruption of aquatic microbial ecosystems

One Health Solution: Integrated approach to regulate antibiotic use in humans, animals, and agriculture while improving wastewater treatment.

India is one of the world’s largest consumers of antibiotics in livestock production. Studies have detected resistance genes in rivers, lakes, and even treated drinking water. The One Health approach mandates coordinated action across healthcare, veterinary services, and environmental sectors.

India’s National One Health Mission

Vision and Goals

Vision Statement: “To build an integrated disease control and pandemic preparedness system in India by bringing human, animal and environmental sectors together for better health outcomes, improved productivity and conservation of biodiversity.”

Initiated: Based on PM-STIAC’s (Prime Minister’s Science, Technology & Innovation Advisory Council) recommendation

Lead Agencies: - Ministry of Health & Family Welfare (MoHFW) - Ministry of Environment, Forest & Climate Change (MoEFCC) - Department of Animal Husbandry & Dairying (DAHD) - AYUSH, DHR, DBT, NCDC, DRDO

Source: ICMR National One Health Mission, 2024

The mission represents India’s commitment to collaborative health security. It addresses gaps in existing One Health activities and coordinates previously siloed efforts across ministries.

Six Focus Areas of India’s One Health Mission

Strategic Priorities

1. Zoonotic Disease Surveillance - Integrated disease surveillance at human-animal-environment interface - Early detection of emerging pathogens

2. Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) Control - Regulate antimicrobial use across sectors - Surveillance of resistance patterns

3. Food Safety - Farm-to-fork safety measures - Quality assurance of animal-origin foods

4. Laboratory Capacity Building - BSL-3/4 laboratory network for high-risk pathogens - Capacity for rapid outbreak response

5. Environmental Health Monitoring - Water quality surveillance - Air pollution and vector control

6. Research & Development - Vaccine platforms and diagnostics - Novel pathogen discovery using AI, NGS, metagenomics

Each focus area requires cross-sector collaboration. Environmental health monitoring, particularly water quality surveillance, is central to preventing disease transmission at the human-animal-environment interface.

One Health Mission: Key Initiatives

Implementation Strategies

Governance Structure:

  • Executive Steering Committee: Chaired by Health & Family Welfare Minister
  • Scientific Steering Committee: Led by Principal Scientific Advisor
  • Cross-Sector Coordination: Representation from all stakeholder ministries

Budget Allocation: ₹386.86 crore for disease control, R&D, and global collaboration

Key Programs:

  1. BSL-3/4 Lab Network: National network for investigating emerging pathogens
  2. Integrated Surveillance: Technology-enabled monitoring across habitats
  3. Pandemic Preparedness: Under PM-ABHIM (Ayushman Bharat Health Infrastructure Mission)
  4. Digital Integration: AI and GIS-based zoonotic surveillance platforms

Source: PMF IAS, National One Health Mission Report, 2024

The mission integrates with existing programs like Swachh Bharat Mission (sanitation), JalJeevan Mission (safe water), and National Animal Disease Control Program, demonstrating comprehensive One Health implementation.

One Health Index for India

Measuring Performance

What is the One Health Index?

A composite metric assessing state-level capacity and performance across 13 key indicators related to human, animal, and environmental health integration.

13 Key Indicators Include:

  • Zoonotic disease surveillance infrastructure
  • Laboratory diagnostic capacity (BSL-2/3 labs)
  • Veterinary public health workforce
  • Water quality monitoring systems
  • AMR surveillance programs
  • Inter-sectoral coordination mechanisms
  • Environmental health regulations
  • Wildlife health monitoring
  • Food safety systems
  • Outbreak response capacity
  • Research & innovation output
  • Community awareness programs
  • Data sharing & integration platforms

Source: One Health Index Calculator for India (ICMR, 2024)

The index helps identify gaps and prioritize resource allocation across states. It enables evidence-based policymaking and tracks progress toward One Health goals.

WASH + One Health Integration

Water, Sanitation, Hygiene under One Health

Why WASH is Central to One Health:

WASH interventions prevent disease transmission across the human-animal-environment interface:

  • Safe Water: Prevents waterborne zoonoses (leptospirosis, cryptosporidiosis)
  • Sanitation: Breaks fecal-oral transmission of pathogens from humans and animals
  • Hygiene: Reduces direct contact with animal reservoirs and contaminated surfaces

One Health WASH Framework:

  1. Integrated Water Quality Monitoring: Test for human AND animal pathogens
  2. Livestock Waste Management: Prevent contamination of water sources
  3. Community Hygiene: Educate on zoonotic disease prevention
  4. Environmental Sanitation: Protect ecosystems supporting disease vectors

Source: PLOS Water Journal, “Towards a One Health approach to WASH”, 2024

Traditional WASH programs focus on human health. One Health WASH expands this to include animal health (veterinary WASH) and environmental health (ecosystem protection), recognizing that 80% of rural households in India live with livestock.

Water Quality Surveillance: A One Health Application

Integrated Monitoring Systems

Conventional Approach: - Monitor water for human pathogens only (E. coli, coliforms) - Reactive response after disease outbreaks

One Health Approach: - Monitor for human, animal, and environmental health indicators - Proactive surveillance for early warning

Example: Wastewater-Based Epidemiology (WBE)

# One Health Surveillance Data Integration
surveillance_data <- data.frame(
  Indicator = c("E. coli", "Leptospira", "Antibiotic Residues", 
                "Cryptosporidium", "Nitrate Levels"),
  Human_Health = c("High", "Medium", "High", "High", "High"),
  Animal_Health = c("Medium", "High", "High", "High", "Medium"),
  Env_Health = c("Medium", "Medium", "High", "Low", "High")
)

Application: COVID-19 wastewater surveillance detected viral RNA before clinical cases emerged, demonstrating the power of environmental monitoring for human disease.

Wastewater surveillance is a quintessential One Health tool. It monitors human pathogens (COVID, polio), animal pathogens (avian influenza), and environmental contaminants (AMR genes, heavy metals) simultaneously, providing early warning for public health interventions.

Case Study: Cholera Outbreaks and One Health

The Human-Animal-Environment Nexus

Background: Cholera (Vibrio cholerae) outbreaks in India often correlate with monsoon flooding and contaminated water sources.

One Health Analysis:

Component Contributing Factors
Human Poor sanitation, open defecation, crowded slums
Animal Aquatic reservoirs (fish, shellfish), livestock near water sources
Environment Flooding, water stagnation, temperature rise, algal blooms

One Health Interventions:

  1. Human: Oral rehydration therapy, vaccination campaigns, hygiene education
  2. Animal: Monitor aquatic animal health, regulate fish farming practices
  3. Environment: Improve drainage, protect water sources, climate adaptation

Outcome: Integrated approach reduces cholera incidence by 60-70% compared to single-sector interventions.

The 2010 Haiti cholera outbreak demonstrated the consequences of ignoring the environment pillar. Contaminated river water (environmental failure) mixed with poor sanitation (human failure) caused a massive epidemic. One Health demands simultaneous action across all three sectors.

One Health in Climate Change Context

Emerging Challenges

Climate-Water-Health Linkages:

  • Extreme Weather: Floods contaminate water sources, droughts concentrate pathogens
  • Temperature Rise: Expands vector habitats (mosquitoes, snails) in water bodies
  • Altered Rainfall: Disrupts seasonal water availability, increases contamination
  • Ecosystem Disruption: Changes animal migration patterns, spreads diseases

One Health Climate Adaptation:

India-Specific Impacts: - Increased dengue/malaria in previously non-endemic areas - More frequent waterborne disease outbreaks post-floods - Climate refugees migrating to areas with poor WASH infrastructure

Climate change is a threat multiplier for One Health. India experienced record floods in 2023-2024, leading to major waterborne disease outbreaks. One Health preparedness requires climate-resilient WASH infrastructure and early warning systems linking meteorological and epidemiological data.
