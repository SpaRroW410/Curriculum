Explanation: The 1972 UN Conference on the Human Environment (Stockholm) brought global attention to environmental hazards affecting human health, leading to increased focus on pollution control, conservation, and sustainable development.

  • Epidemiologist
  • Public health engineer

Explanation: While veterinarians are critical for One Health (Module 1), traditional environmental health teams include public health physicians, epidemiologists, engineers, town planners, sociologists, economists, and health inspectors. Question 3

Water quantity is as important as water quality because:

  • Large volume dilutes contaminants
  • Adequate water enables hygiene practices like hand washing
  • People drink more water when it’s available
  • Storage tanks require significant volumes

Explanation: Without abundant water near the home, hygiene becomes impossible. Hand washing after defecation, bathing, and cleaning require sufficient water quantity. A family conserves water if the source is far, compromising hygiene even if the water is microbiologically safe.

WHO Guidelines for Drinking Water Quality

Overview and Evolution

Purpose:

Define standards for countries to develop national regulations ensuring safe drinking water supplies.

Publication History:

  • 1984: First edition published
  • 1993: Vol. 1 of 2nd edition
  • 1996: Vol. 2 of 2nd edition
  • 2011: Vol. 1 & II of 3rd edition ⭐ (current standard)
  • 2017: Updated addendum

Key Principle:

Standards must consider local environmental, social, economic, and cultural contexts—not one-size-fits-all.

Four Aspects of Water Quality:

  1. Acceptability aspects (Physical + Inorganic)
  2. Microbiological aspects
  3. Chemical aspects (Inorganic + Organic + Pesticides)
  4. Radiological aspects

The WHO guidelines are recommendations, not mandatory regulations. Each country adapts them based on local conditions, resources, and priorities. India’s standards (IS 10500) largely align with WHO but have some variations based on endemic conditions (e.g., fluoride).

I. Acceptability Aspects

A. Physical Parameters

Definition: Characteristics affecting water’s aesthetic quality and acceptability to consumers.

Key Parameters:

Parameter Acceptable Limit Health/Aesthetic Impact
Turbidity <5 NTU (Nephelometric Turbidity Units) >5 NTU visible cloudiness, shields pathogens from disinfection
Colour <15 TCU (True Colour Units) Indicates organic matter, iron, or manganese contamination
Taste and Odour No objectionable taste/odour May indicate organic decay, hydrogen sulfide, or chlorination by-products
Temperature <25°C High temp reduces dissolved oxygen, increases microbial growth

Significance:

Even if microbiologically safe, water with poor acceptability aspects may cause consumers to seek unsafe alternative sources.

Turbidity is critical—particles shield bacteria from chlorine disinfection. Studies show that reducing turbidity from 5 NTU to 1 NTU can increase disinfection efficacy by 90%. Consumers often prefer clear water even if unsafe, rejecting turbid water even if treated.

B. Inorganic Constituents (Acceptability)

Affect taste, odor, and appliance function but not directly toxic at typical levels:

Constituent Limit (mg/L) Effect
Chlorides <250 Salty taste, corrosion
Hardness (CaCO₃) <200 (soft), >500 (very hard) Scaling in pipes/appliances, soap inefficiency
Ammonia <1.5 Indicator of recent organic pollution
pH 6.5 - 8.5 <6.5 corrosive; >8.5 bitter taste, scaling
Hydrogen sulphide <0.05 Rotten egg odor
Iron <0.3 Stains laundry, metallic taste
Sodium <200 Salty taste
Sulphate <250 Laxative effect at high levels
Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) <500 (acceptable), <1000 (permissible) Affects taste
Zinc <3 Metallic taste
Manganese <0.1 Brown stains
Dissolved Oxygen >5 Indicates water freshness
Copper <2 Metallic taste, blue-green stains
Aluminium <0.2 Alzheimer’s risk debated

TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) is often mistaken as a health parameter. While very high TDS affects taste, moderate TDS is not harmful and may provide essential minerals. Reverse osmosis systems removing all TDS may produce water lacking beneficial minerals.

II. Microbiological Aspects

Bacteriological Indicators

Why Indicators?

Testing for all pathogens is impractical. Indicators signal fecal contamination and potential presence of pathogens.

Primary Indicator: Coliform Organisms

  • Total Coliforms: Gram-negative, lactose-fermenting bacteria (includes E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter)
  • Fecal Coliforms (Thermotolerant): Ferment lactose at 44°C; E. coli is the primary member
  • Escherichia coli: Definitive indicator of recent fecal contamination

Guideline: - Drinking water: 0 E. coli or fecal coliforms per 100 mL - Treated water entering distribution: 0 coliforms per 100 mL - Treated water in distribution: <3 coliforms per 100 mL (if chlorine residual present)

Other Indicators:

  • Faecal Streptococci (Enterococcus): More persistent than coliforms, indicates older contamination
  • Clostridium perfringens: Spore-forming; indicates remote or intermittent contamination

E. coli is the gold standard fecal indicator because: (1) exclusively of fecal origin, (2) doesn’t multiply in water, (3) easy to detect, (4) outnumbers pathogens 1000:1. However, it doesn’t indicate protozoa or viruses, which are more chlorine-resistant.

Virological and Biological Aspects

Virological Aspects:

Waterborne viruses are major concern: - Enteroviruses: Poliovirus, Coxsackievirus, Echovirus - Hepatitis A and E viruses: Fecal-oral transmission - Rotavirus: Leading cause of infant diarrhea - Norovirus: Gastroenteritis outbreaks - Adenovirus: Respiratory and gastro symptoms

Challenge: Viruses are smaller than bacteria, more resistant to chlorine, and present in lower numbers—difficult to detect directly.

Approach: If E. coli is absent and water is adequately disinfected, viral risk is considered low.

Biological Aspects:

  • Protozoan cysts: Giardia (cysts), Cryptosporidium (oocysts)—highly resistant to chlorine
  • Helminth ova: Roundworm, hookworm eggs—removed by filtration

Guideline: <1 Giardia cyst or Cryptosporidium oocyst per 10L in treated water

The 1993 Milwaukee Cryptosporidium outbreak (400,000 ill, 100 deaths) occurred despite meeting bacterial standards. Cryptosporidium oocysts are 10-100× more resistant to chlorine than bacteria. This led to increased focus on protozoa monitoring and use of UV disinfection or filtration.

Arsenic is the #1 chemical contaminant of concern globally. Chronic exposure causes arsenicosis with skin lesions, hyperpigmentation, and cancers. Bangladesh and West Bengal face severe arsenic contamination. India’s permissible limit is 0.01 mg/L (WHO), but 0.05 mg/L in areas without alternatives (IS 10500).

Key Concerns:

  • Persistent in environment
  • Lipophilic (accumulate in fatty tissues)
  • Many are carcinogenic or mutagenic
  • Enter water via industrial discharge, landfill leachate, gasoline spills

Vinyl chloride (0.5 µg/L limit) is one of the strictest guidelines because it causes liver angiosarcoma even at very low doses. Benzene (10 µg/L) is a known human carcinogen causing leukemia. These compounds rarely occur naturally—their presence always indicates industrial contamination.

Most organochlorine pesticides (DDT, Aldrin, Dieldrin, Chlordane) are banned or restricted due to persistence, bioaccumulation, and toxicity. However, they remain in soil and water decades after application. India banned DDT for agriculture (1989) but allowed it for malaria control until 2020. Monitoring is critical as old stocks may still contaminate water.

Polynuclear Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs)

Coal-Tar Contamination and Cancer Risk

What are PAHs?

Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons formed during incomplete combustion of organic materials: - Coal, oil, wood, garbage burning - Vehicle exhaust - Industrial processes - Cigarette smoke

Major PAHs in Water:

  • Benzo[a]pyrene (BaP): Most potent carcinogen, guideline = 0.7 µg/L
  • Benzo[b]fluoranthene, Benzo[k]fluoranthene
  • Indeno[1,2,3-cd]pyrene
  • Dibenz[a,h]anthracene

WHO Recommendations for PAH Control:

  1. Turbidity Reduction: PAHs adsorb to suspended particles. Reducing turbidity removes PAHs.

  2. Ban Coal-Tar Materials: Discontinue use of coal-tar-based materials for pipe lining and storage tank coatings—major contamination source.

  3. Source Identification: When PAH contamination detected, identify specific compounds and sources, as carcinogenic potential varies.

The 1970s-80s saw widespread PAH contamination from coal-tar pipe linings in U.S. and Europe. Decades later, elevated cancer rates appeared. WHO recommends removing old coal-tar pipes. In India, some old municipal systems still have coal-tar linings, requiring monitoring.

Health-Risk Assessment Framework

Understanding TDI, ADI, NOAEL, LOAEL

Why Risk Assessment?

Cannot test every chemical on humans ethically. Must extrapolate from animal studies to establish safe limits.

Key Concepts:

1. Tolerable Daily Intake (TDI)

  • Maximum amount of a substance a person can consume daily over a lifetime without appreciable health risk
  • Expressed as mg/kg body weight/day

2. Acceptable Daily Intake (ADI)

  • Similar to TDI but used for food additives and pesticides (intentional additions)
  • TDI used for contaminants (unintentional)

3. No-Observed-Adverse-Effect Level (NOAEL)

  • Highest dose in animal studies where NO adverse effects detected
  • Basis for deriving TDI

4. Lowest-Observed-Adverse-Effect Level (LOAEL)

  • Lowest dose in animal studies where adverse effects ARE detected
  • Used when NOAEL data unavailable (with additional uncertainty factor)

Example: Arsenic NOAEL study on rats might find 0.3 mg/kg bw/day causes no tumors. But humans may be more sensitive, so we apply uncertainty factors (typically 100-1000) to derive a safe TDI. This conservative approach protects public health given scientific uncertainties.

Uncertainty Factors (UF)

Accounting for Variability and Unknowns

Purpose:

Account for uncertainties when extrapolating from animal studies to human populations.

Four Sources of Uncertainty:

Source Uncertainty Factor Range Rationale
Interspecies variation 1 - 10 Animals may respond differently than humans
Intraspecies variation 1 - 10 Humans vary (children, elderly, sick more sensitive)
Adequacy of studies 1 - 10 Short-term study → lifetime exposure extrapolation
Nature/severity of effect 1 - 10 Severe effects (cancer) require more caution

Total UF Calculation:

UF (total) = UF₁ × UF₂ × UF₃ × UF₄

Typical Values:

  • 100: Standard for well-studied chemicals (10 × 10: interspecies × intraspecies)
  • 1000: Additional factor when using LOAEL instead of NOAEL
  • 10,000: Maximum; higher values make TDI too imprecise (guideline becomes “provisional”)

Example: Arsenic uses UF=100 (10 interspecies × 10 intraspecies) because we have good human epidemiological data. Lead uses higher UF because children are especially vulnerable (neurodevelopment). The WHO expert panel determines UF by consensus, reviewing all available evidence.

Deriving Guideline Values Using TDI

Step-by-Step Calculation

Formula 1: Calculate TDI

TDI (mg/kg bw/day) = NOAEL (or LOAEL) / UF

Formula 2: Calculate Guideline Value (GV)

GV (mg/L) = (TDI × bw × P) / C

Where:

  • bw = Body weight (60 kg adult, 10 kg child, 5 kg infant)
  • P = Fraction of TDI allocated to drinking water (typically 0.1 or 10%)
  • C = Daily drinking water consumption (2 L adult, 1 L child, 0.75 L infant)

Why P = 0.1?

Most chemicals come from multiple sources (food, air, soil). Water typically contributes 10-20% of total exposure. For chemicals exclusively in water (e.g., disinfection by-products), P may be higher (0.8).

The allocation factor P is critical. For lead, P=0.5 (50%) because drinking water is a major source for infants. For pesticides, P=0.1 (10%) because food is the primary source. When deriving guideline values, we must consider all exposure routes, not just water.

Worked Example: Arsenic Guideline Value

Applying the TDI Approach

Given Data:

  • NOAEL (from animal studies) = 0.3 mg/kg bw/day
  • Uncertainty Factor (UF) = 100 (10 interspecies × 10 intraspecies)
  • Allocation to water (P) = 0.1 (10%)
  • Body weight (bw) = 60 kg (adult)
  • Water consumption (C) = 2 L/day

Step 1: Calculate TDI

TDI <- NOAEL / UF
TDI <- 0.3 / 100
TDI <- 0.003 mg/kg bw/day

Step 2: Calculate Guideline Value

GV <- (TDI * bw * P) / C
GV <- (0.003 * 60 * 0.1) / 2
GV <- 0.018 / 2
GV <- 0.009 mg/L = 9 µg/L

WHO Guideline: 0.01 mg/L (10 µg/L) — rounded for practical monitoring

WHO’s arsenic guideline (10 µg/L) is based on epidemiological studies in Bangladesh, Taiwan, and Chile showing skin lesions and cancers. The calculation here is simplified; actual derivation considered multiple studies, exposure routes, and cancer risk models. Some countries (Bangladesh) use 50 µg/L as an interim target due to widespread natural contamination.

IV. Radiological Aspects

Radioactivity in Drinking Water

What is Radioactivity?

Nuclear disintegration of unstable isotopes, releasing ionizing radiation (alpha, beta, gamma particles).

Unit of Activity:

  • Becquerel (Bq): 1 disintegration per second (modern unit)
  • Curie (Ci): 3.7 × 10¹⁰ disintegrations per second (old unit)
  • 1 Ci = 37 GBq

WHO Guideline Values:

Indicator Guideline Value Rationale
Gross Alpha Activity 0.5 Bq/L Screening for α-emitters (radon, radium, uranium)
Gross Beta Activity 1.0 Bq/L Screening for β-emitters (cesium-137, strontium-90, tritium)

If screening levels exceeded: Identify specific radionuclides and assess individual dose contribution.

Radiological contamination comes from natural (uranium, radium in rocks) or anthropogenic (nuclear facilities, medical waste) sources. Alpha emitters are more biologically damaging than beta/gamma due to high energy in short range. Radon (gas) from uranium decay in groundwater is a common concern in granite regions.

Radiological Health Effects

Somatic vs. Hereditary, Stochastic vs. Non-Stochastic

Types of Effects:

1. Somatic Effects (manifest in exposed individual) - Acute: Radiation sickness, burns (high dose, short time) - Delayed: Cancer, cataracts, cardiovascular disease

2. Hereditary Effects (affect descendants) - Genetic mutations passed to offspring - Risk increases with gonadal dose

Dose-Response Relationships:

Stochastic Effects: - Probability increases with dose (no threshold) - Severity constant - Example: Cancer risk (0.05% per mSv for solid cancers)

Non-Stochastic (Deterministic) Effects: - Severity increases with dose (threshold exists) - Example: Cataracts (threshold ~2 Gy), skin burns

Radiation Protection Principle:

Keep exposures As Low As Reasonably Achievable (ALARA), even below guideline limits.

There’s no “safe” dose for stochastic effects (cancer)—risk exists at any exposure. Guideline values represent acceptable risk (typically <10⁻⁴ additional lifetime cancer risk). Non-stochastic effects have thresholds; below threshold, no effect occurs. Water guidelines aim to prevent deterministic effects and minimize stochastic risk.

Module 3 Assessment: MCQs (Current Module)

Question 1

The WHO guideline value for arsenic in drinking water is:

  • 0.05 mg/L
  • 0.01 mg/L
  • 0.001 mg/L
  • 0.1 mg/L

Explanation: WHO guideline for arsenic is 0.01 mg/L (10 µg/L). This is based on cancer risk assessment from epidemiological studies. India also adopted 0.01 mg/L, though some arsenic-affected areas use 0.05 mg/L as interim target.

Question 2

Which of the following is a water quality indicator used to detect REMOTE or intermittent fecal contamination?

  • E. coli
  • Total coliforms
  • Clostridium perfringens
  • Fecal streptococci

Explanation: Clostridium perfringens forms spores that survive harsh conditions and persist longer than vegetative bacteria. Its presence indicates remote or intermittent contamination. E. coli indicates recent contamination; it doesn’t survive long in water.

Question 3

The formula for calculating Tolerable Daily Intake (TDI) is:

  • TDI = NOAEL × UF
  • TDI = NOAEL / UF
  • TDI = (NOAEL / UF) × body weight
  • TDI = NOAEL - UF

Explanation: TDI = NOAEL / UF (or LOAEL / UF if NOAEL unavailable). We divide by uncertainty factor to account for inter/intraspecies variation and study limitations. Example: NOAEL 0.3 mg/kg/day, UF 100 → TDI = 0.003 mg/kg/day.

Question 4

Gross alpha activity in drinking water should not exceed:

  • 0.1 Bq/L
  • 0.5 Bq/L
  • 1.0 Bq/L
  • 5.0 Bq/L

Explanation: WHO guideline: Gross alpha activity <0.5 Bq/L, Gross beta activity <1.0 Bq/L. These are screening levels; if exceeded, identify specific radionuclides (radon, radium, uranium, cesium-137, strontium-90) and assess individual doses.

Question 5 (Application-Based)

A contaminant has NOAEL = 0.5 mg/kg bw/day, UF = 100, P = 0.1, bw = 60 kg, C = 2 L/day. Calculate the guideline value:

  • 0.05 mg/L
  • 0.025 mg/L
  • 0.015 mg/L
  • 0.005 mg/L

Explanation: TDI = 0.5/100 = 0.005 mg/kg/day GV = (0.005 × 60 × 0.1) / 2 = 0.03 / 2 = 0.015 mg/L = 15 µg/L This demonstrates application of the TDI derivation formula.
