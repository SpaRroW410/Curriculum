Pollution Indicators

What Are Pollution Indicators?

Definition: Measurable parameters that reflect water contamination status

Categories of Indicators:

  1. Physical Indicators - Turbidity, color, temperature
  2. Chemical Indicators - BOD, nitrogen, phosphorus, chlorides
  3. Microbiological Indicators - Coliform organisms, fecal streptococci
  4. Biological Indicators - Aquatic organism presence/absence

Physical Pollution Indicators

Visible Water Quality Parameters

Turbidity: - Suspended solids causing cloudiness - Measured in NTU (Nephelometric Turbidity Units) - WHO guideline: <1 NTU for drinking water - Indicates: Sediment, algae, pathogens likely

Color: - Measured in TCU (True Color Units) or Hazen units - Drinking water guideline: <5 TCU - Natural colors: Tannins, iron (brown), algae (green) - Artificial colors: Dyes, industrial chemicals

Temperature: - WHO guideline: <25°C preferred - Indicates: Shallow contamination sources - High temp favors bacterial growth

Taste & Odor: - Subjective but important - Indicates: Chemical contamination, algal growth - Affects public acceptance of water

Chemical Pollution Indicators

Measuring Organic & Inorganic Load

Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD): - Measures organic matter decomposition - High BOD: Indicates organic pollution - Bacteria consume dissolved oxygen - Can lead to anoxic conditions (fish kills) - Guideline: <5 mg/L

Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD): - Measures all oxidizable matter - Includes recalcitrant compounds - Often COD > BOD - Higher priority pollutant indicator

Dissolved Oxygen (DO): - Essential for aquatic life - Low DO: Indicates decomposition - Fish need: >4-5 mg/L - Anoxia: <2 mg/L leads to deaths

Nitrogen & Phosphorus: - Excess causes eutrophication - Algal blooms result - Reduces sunlight penetration - Depletes oxygen

Microbiological Indicators

Detecting Bacterial Contamination

Coliform Organisms: - Gram-negative bacteria (E. coli family) - Natural habitat: Animal intestines - Presence indicates: Fecal contamination - Drinking water standard: Zero (absent in 100 mL)

Fecal Coliform (E. coli): - More specific to fecal contamination - Human/animal gut bacteria - Indicates: Recent/direct contamination - Drinking water standard: Absent

Fecal Streptococci: - Human/animal intestinal bacteria - Indicates: Fecal contamination - More resistant than E. coli - Used to distinguish human vs animal sources

Clostridium Perfringens: - Spore-forming bacterium - Survives water treatment better than vegetative bacteria - Indicates: Remote or past contamination - Used for treatment efficacy assessment

Microbiological Testing Methods

Laboratory Detection Techniques

Presumptive Coliform Test: - Multiple tube method (traditional) - Tubes with lactose broth media - Gas production indicates coliforms - Confirmation needed for identification

Confirmatory Tests: - EMB (Eosin-Methylene Blue) agar plates - Gram staining - Culture isolation - Biochemical identification

Membrane Filtration Technique: - Water filtered through 0.45 μm membrane - Membrane placed on selective media - Colonies counted after incubation - Modern quantitative method - Advantage: Direct count of organisms

Colony Count: - Heterotrophic plate count (HPC) - Total bacteria on plate - Non-specific indicator - High counts suggest general contamination

Water-Related Diseases

Classification Framework

Three Main Categories:

  1. Water-Borne Diseases - Ingestion of contaminated water
  2. Water-Washed Diseases - Poor hygiene due to water scarcity
  3. Water-Based Diseases - Aquatic host involvement

Water-Borne Diseases

Ingestion-Based Transmission

Bacterial Diseases: - Typhoid & Paratyphoid fever (Salmonella typhi/paratyphi) - Bacillary dysentery (Shigella species) - E. coli diarrhea (various pathogenic strains) - Cholera (Vibrio cholerae) - Leptospirosis (Leptospira, from contaminated urine)

Viral Diseases: - Hepatitis A (fecal-oral route) - Hepatitis E (similar transmission) - Poliomyelitis (paralysis risk) - Rotavirus (infant diarrhea)

Protozoal Diseases: - Amoebiasis/Amoebic dysentery (Entamoeba histolytica) - Giardiasis (Giardia lamblia)

Helminthic Diseases: - Roundworm infections (Ascaris) - Threadworm (Enterobius) - Hydatid disease (Echinococcus)

Water-Washed Diseases

Inadequate Water for Hygiene

Why Water Quantity Matters: - Poor hygiene when water scarce - Hand washing after defecation critical - Food preparation hygiene compromised - Personal cleanliness suffers

Associated Diseases: - Trachoma (Chlamydia trachomatis eye infection) - Skin infections (fungi, bacteria) - Scabies (Sarcoptes mite) - Conjunctivitis (bacterial/viral)

Transmission Route: - NOT through contaminated water directly - Through contaminated hands/fomites - Inadequate washing enables transmission

Water-Based Diseases

Aquatic Host Involvement

Snail-Transmitted: - Schistosomiasis (Schistosoma species) - Aquatic snails are intermediate hosts - Cercariae penetrate skin in contaminated water - Chronic parasitic infection

Cyclops-Transmitted (Copepod): - Guinea worm disease (Dracunculus) - Water contains infected copepods - Can be prevented by simple filtration - Significant morbidity (ulceration, pain)

Fish/Crustacean-Transmitted: - Tapeworm infections (Diphyllobothrium) - Fish are intermediate hosts - Undercooked/raw food transmission - Inadequate cooking prevention

Vector-Borne Water-Related Diseases

Insects Breeding in/Near Water

Mosquito-Borne: - Malaria (Plasmodium) - Anopheles mosquito - Dengue fever - Aedes mosquito - Filariasis - Culex mosquito - Breeding in stagnant water

Other Vectors: - Onchocerciasis (river blindness) - Blackflies - African Trypanosomiasis - Tsetse fly near water - Arboviruses - Various water-associated vectors

Prevention Strategy: - Water management (drain stagnant pools) - Vector control - Bug netting/repellents

Burden of Waterborne Diseases

Global & Indian Context

Global Burden: - 2-3 million deaths annually from diarrhea - 663 million lacking safe water access - 2.6 billion without improved sanitation

Indian Burden: - 1.5 million children die of diarrhea annually - 37.7 million cases of waterborne diseases per year - 73 million working days lost annually - ₹600 million economic loss per year

Vulnerable Populations in India: - Rural populations (700 million in 1.42 million villages) - Urban slums (inadequate water/sanitation) - Nomadic groups (no fixed water source) - Children <5 years (highest mortality)

Health Associations: Fluoride

Dental Fluorosis

Protective Dose: - 0.5-0.8 ppm (India) or 1 ppm (temperate climates) - Prevents dental caries - Safe threshold

Excess Fluoride Effects: - >1.0-1.5 ppm: Mild dental mottling - >2.0-3.0 ppm: Moderate fluorosis (enamel defects) - >3.0 ppm: Severe fluorosis (pitting, discoloration)

Skeletal Fluorosis: - Prolonged exposure to excess fluoride - Fluoride accumulates in bones - Causes: Joint stiffness, back pain - Severe: Neurological complications

Current Status in India: - 66 million at risk from excess fluoride - Predominantly in: Andhra Pradesh, Rajasthan, Gujarat, MP - Major public health concern - Mitigation: Defluorination technologies (alum, RO)

Health Associations: Nitrates & Cyanosis

Methaemoglobinaemia in Infants

Mechanism: - High nitrate water (>50 mg/L NO₃) - Nitrate converts to nitrite in gut - Nitrite converts hemoglobin to methaemoglobin - Methaemoglobin cannot carry oxygen - Result: “Blue Baby Syndrome” (cyanosis)

Vulnerable Population: - Infants <6 months (highest risk) - Bottle-fed infants using contaminated water - Older children/adults at lower risk (stomach acidity protection)

Clinical Presentation: - Bluish discoloration of lips, fingers - Shortness of breath - Lethargy - Potentially fatal if untreated

Prevention: - Monitor nitrate levels - Guideline: <50 mg/L - Breastfeeding protective - Treated water essential for bottle-feeding

Health Associations: Arsenic

Chronic Toxicity & Cancer

Excess Arsenic Sources: - Natural geological deposits (Bangladesh, India) - Industrial contamination

Health Effects: - Chronic exposure: Skin lesions, hyperpigmentation - Long-term: Peripheral neuropathy - Carcinogenic: Lung, skin, bladder cancer risk - Latency: 10-20+ years

Current Status in India: - 10 million at risk from excess arsenic - Predominantly: West Bengal, Assam - WHO guideline: 0.01 mg/L (10 μg/L) - Many wells exceed this

Mitigation: - Well switching (find uncontaminated sources) - RO systems (highly effective) - Arsenic removal technologies

Health Associations: Heavy Metals

Lead, Cadmium, Mercury

Lead (Pb): - WHO guideline: 0.01 mg/L (10 μg/L) - Sources: Old pipes, industrial waste - Effects: Neurotoxicity (children), cognitive impairment - Chronic exposure concern

Cadmium (Cd): - WHO guideline: 0.003 mg/L (3 μg/L) - Sources: Batteries, electroplating waste - Effects: Kidney damage, bone loss - Accumulative toxin

Mercury (Hg): - WHO guideline: 0.006 mg/L (6 μg/L) - Sources: Thermometers, industrial processes - Effects: Neurological damage, tremors - Particularly dangerous in fetuses

Iron & Manganese: - Excess causes: Staining, taste issues - Iron >0.3 mg/L: Dark discoloration - Manganese >0.1 mg/L: Neurological concerns (prolonged exposure)

Health Associations: Hardness

Cardiovascular & Other Effects

Water Hardness Definition: - Soap-destroying power of water - Caused by calcium & magnesium salts - Soft water: <150 mg/L CaCO₃ - Hard water: >300 mg/L CaCO₃

Cardiovascular Hypothesis: - Soft water: Increased cardiovascular mortality - Hard water: Protective effect observed - Mechanism: Calcium/magnesium minerals beneficial - Epidemiological associations (not universally proven)

Practical Implications: - Excessive softening may remove protective minerals - Balance needed between hardness removal & mineral retention - WHO does not set upper limit for hardness

Chemical Contamination Categories

WHO Guideline Values Summary

Inorganic Chemicals of Health Significance:

Priority Heavy Metals: - Antimony: 0.02 mg/L - Arsenic: 0.01 mg/L - Cadmium: 0.003 mg/L - Chromium (total): 0.05 mg/L - Lead: 0.01 mg/L - Mercury: 0.006 mg/L - Nickel: 0.07 mg/L - Selenium: 0.04 mg/L

Anions of Concern: - Chlorides: No health limit (taste limit: 250 mg/L) - Nitrate (as NO₃): 50 mg/L - Nitrite (as NO₂): 3 mg/L - Fluoride: 1.5 mg/L

Organic Chemical Contaminants

Complex Synthetic Compounds

Chlorinated Compounds: - Carbon tetrachloride: 2 μg/L - Dichloromethane: 20 μg/L - Vinyl chloride: 5 μg/L - 1,1-Dichloroethane: 30 μg/L - 1,2-Dichloroethene: 50 μg/L

Aromatic Hydrocarbons: - Benzene: 10 μg/L - Toluene: 700 μg/L - Xylenes: 500 μg/L - Ethylbenzene: 300 μg/L - Styrene: 20 μg/L

Polynuclear Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAH): - Benzo(a)pyrene: 0.7 μg/L - Carcinogenic potential - Coal-tar pipe materials: Potential source - Treatment for turbidity reduction helps control PAH

Pesticide Guideline Values

Agricultural Chemical Contamination

Organochlorine Pesticides: - Aldrin/Dieldrin: 0.03 μg/L - Chlordane: 0.2 μg/L - DDT: 2 μg/L - Heptachlor & heptachlor epoxide: 0.03 μg/L - Hexachlorobenzene: 1 μg/L - Lindane: 2 μg/L - Methoxychlor: 20 μg/L - Pentachlorophenol: 9 μg/L

Herbicide Limits: - 2,4-D: 30 μg/L - Diquat: 30 μg/L - MCPA: 2 μg/L

Concerns: - Bioaccumulation in aquatic organisms - Persistence in environment - Long-term health effects (cancer risk)

Radiological Aspects

Radiation in Drinking Water

Background Radioactivity: - Naturally occurring radionuclides (uranium, radium) - Human-made radioactivity (industrial accidents)

WHO Guideline Values: - Gross alpha activity: 0.5 Bq/L - Gross beta activity: 1.0 Bq/L - Becquerel (Bq): Unit of radioactivity (disintegrations per second)

Health Effects: - Stochastic effects: Cancer risk (no safe threshold) - Somatic effects: Direct tissue damage - Long-term chronic exposure concern - Regulatory limits set conservatively

Testing & Monitoring: - Specialized laboratories required - Geological area assessment - Rare in most drinking water sources - More common in uranium mining regions

Pollution & Legal Framework

India’s Water Pollution Control Laws

Water Prevention and Control of Pollution Act (1974): - Comprehensive legislation for water pollution control - Establishes Central & State Water Boards - Joint Water Boards for inter-state waters - Enforcement authority for pollution control

Key Provisions: - Standards setting: Drinking water quality standards - Monitoring: Regular water quality surveillance - Enforcement: Penalties for pollution - Permits: Required for industrial discharge - Adjudication: Courts to settle disputes

Constitution Article 47: - Duty of State to provide clean drinking water - Priority in health & sanitation - Legal obligation of government

National Water Policy: - Framework for water resource management - Equitable distribution principles - Pollution prevention measures

Sources of Water Pollution

Industrial, Agricultural & Domestic

Industrial Sources: - Factory wastewater (heavy metals, chemicals) - Mining operations (sulfides, acids) - Textile mills (dyes, organic compounds) - Pharmaceutical waste - Petroleum refining

Agricultural Sources: - Pesticide runoff - Fertilizer nitrogen & phosphorus (eutrophication) - Animal waste (coliforms, pathogens) - Herbicide residues

Domestic Sources: - Sewage (organic matter, pathogens) - Detergents (phosphates, surfactants) - Kitchen waste disposal - Gray water (bathing, cleaning)

Other Sources: - Urban runoff (oils, heavy metals) - Road salt (chlorides) - Atmospheric deposition - Landfill leachate

Self-Purification in Rivers

Natural Dilution & Degradation

Natural Purification Mechanisms: 1. Dilution - Pollutants become dispersed 2. Sedimentation - Particles settle 3. Aeration - Water oxygenation 4. Oxidation - Chemical decomposition 5. Sunlight - Bacterial/chemical breakdown 6. Aquatic life - Organisms consume organics

Limitations: - Insufficient for high pollution loads - Takes time (24-72 hours typical) - Completely overwhelmed in severely polluted rivers - Example: Indian rivers (Yamuna, Ganges) require extensive treatment

Urban vs Industrial Water Pollution

Context Matters

Urban Pollution (Dilute): - Sewage from residential areas - Moderate contaminant levels - Typically: Organic matter, coliforms - Treatment: Standard water works sufficient

Industrial Pollution (Concentrated): - Chemical factories, refineries, textiles - High levels of toxic substances - Persistent organic pollutants - Treatment: Advanced methods (activated carbon, RO, oxidation)

Mixed Urban-Industrial: - Cities with industries - Combined sewage overflows - Variable pollution loads - Requires flexible treatment

Health Impact Assessment

Evaluating Disease Risk

Dose-Response Relationship: - Tolerable Daily Intake (TDI): Safe chronic exposure - Acceptable Daily Intake (ADI): Non-carcinogen daily safe dose - No-Observed-Adverse-Effect Level (NOAEL): Below effect threshold - Lowest-Observed-Adverse-Effect Level (LOAEL): Lowest harmful dose

Uncertainty Factors: - Applied to NOAEL to determine safe standards - Accounts for: Inter-species variation, human sensitivity, data gaps - Typical UF: 100-1000x (human variation: 10x, species: 10x)

Example Calculation: - Animal NOAEL: 100 mg/kg/day - Uncertainty factor: 1000 - Human ADI: 0.1 mg/kg/day

Guideline Value Derivation: - Based on TDI/ADI - Accounts for water consumption (2L/day typical) - Often 1-2% of ADI allocated to drinking water

Prevention & Mitigation

Addressing Pollution Sources

Source Control: - Industrial discharge permits & standards - Wastewater treatment requirements - Agricultural best practices (reduced fertilizer) - Landfill siting away from water sources

Water Treatment Technologies: - Activated carbon (organic compounds) - Reverse osmosis (dissolved salts) - Advanced oxidation (persistent chemicals) - Ion exchange (hardness removal)

Surveillance & Monitoring: - Regular water quality testing - Early warning systems - Health surveillance for disease patterns - Chemical monitoring for emerging contaminants

Public Health Measures: - Education on water safety - Boiling recommendations - Distribution of household filters - Vaccination (hepatitis A, typhoid where applicable)

Case Study: Fluorosis Mitigation

Problem Village Scenario

Context: Village with 5,000 people, 66 mg/L fluoride (extremely high)

Health Impact: - High prevalence of dental fluorosis - Some skeletal fluorosis in elderly - Public health concern

Solutions: 1. Well switching: Find uncontaminated wells (if available) 2. Defluorination: Alum precipitation, activated alumina, RO 3. Dietary: Limited tea consumption (high fluoride source) 4. Health education: Awareness of risk, mitigation strategies

Cost-Benefit: - Well switching: Cheapest if alternative exists - RO: Most effective, requires electricity - Community-level: Shared treatment system most sustainable

Case Study: Arsenic Contamination

West Bengal & Assam Example

Problem: - Groundwater naturally high in arsenic - 10 million people at risk - No color/taste indication (hidden hazard)

Manifestations: - Skin lesions (hyperkeratosis) - Gangrene in severe cases - Cancer risk (lungs, skin, bladder)

Interventions: 1. Well selection: Identify clean wells 2. RO systems: 99.9% arsenic removal 3. Alternative sources: Surface water (if available) 4. Health monitoring: Screen exposed populations

Ongoing Challenge: - Limited alternative sources in many areas - Cost of RO systems prohibitive for poorest - Long latency of health effects makes monitoring difficult

Summary: Pollution & Diseases

Key Takeaways

Pollution Indicators: - Physical (turbidity, color) - Chemical (BOD, metals) - Microbiological (coliforms)

Disease Categories: - Water-borne (ingestion) - Water-washed (hygiene) - Water-based (aquatic host)

Critical Health Links: - Fluoride → Dental fluorosis - Arsenic → Cancer risk - Nitrates → Cyanosis in infants - Microbes → Diarrhea & death
