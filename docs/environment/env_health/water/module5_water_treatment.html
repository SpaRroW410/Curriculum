Treatment Overview

Purpose of Water Treatment

Primary Goal: Convert raw water into safe, wholesome water suitable for drinking

Three Main Objectives: 1. Remove Physical Impurities (sediment, turbidity) 2. Eliminate Biological Threats (bacteria, viruses, protozoa) 3. Reduce Chemical Contaminants (heavy metals, salts)

Standards: Meet WHO guidelines and national drinking water standards

Stage 1: Storage & Sedimentation

Purpose & Process

Primary Benefits: - Removes ~90% suspended impurities in 24 hours - Allows time for settling under gravity - Reduces load on downstream filters - Cost-effective natural purification

Physical Changes: - Clear water formation (better light penetration) - Turbidity reduction - Improved conditions for UV disinfection

Storage: Chemical Changes

Oxidation & Bacterial Reduction

Chemical Processes: - Aerobic bacteria oxidize organic matter - Free ammonia converts to nitrates - Dissolved oxygen is consumed - pH stabilizes

Microbiological Purification: - Total bacterial count drops by 90% in 5-7 days - Pathogenic organisms gradually die out - Viruses show significant reduction - Optimal storage period: 10-14 days (river water)

⚠️ Caution: - Prolonged storage (>14 days) allows algae growth - Bad odors and color develop - Balance needed between purification and degradation

Storage Infrastructure

Impounding Reservoirs & Tanks

Impounding Reservoirs: - Large earthwork or masonry structures - Store significant water volumes - Suitable for urban systems - Cities: Mumbai, Chennai, Nagpur

Household/Community Tanks: - Overhead ground-level storage - Rooftop collection systems - Simple gravity-fed distribution - Requires protection from contamination

Key Requirement: Covered storage to prevent re-contamination

Stage 2: Filtration - Overview

Two Main Filtration Types

Slow Sand Filter (SSF)

  • Biological process
  • Gravity-driven
  • No chemicals needed
  • Low maintenance
  • Ideal for rural areas

Rapid Sand Filter (RSF)

  • Mechanical process
  • Chemical pre-treatment required
  • Regular backwashing needed
  • Higher operational cost
  • Best for urban systems

Key Principle: Filtration removes 98-99% of bacteria

SSF: Slow Sand Filtration

Operating Characteristics

Flow Rate: 0.1-0.4 mm/hour (very slow)

Sand Specifications: - Grain size: 0.2-0.4 mm (fine sand) - Depth: 0.5-1.5 meters - Supported by gravel layer (30-40 cm) - Total surface area: ~15,000 m² per m³

Filtration Duration: 24-72 hours for water to pass through

Pathogen Removal: 90-99% bacterial reduction

SSF: The Schmutzdecke Layer

Biological Magic

What is Schmutzdecke? - Biologically active layer at sand surface - Forms naturally within 2-3 weeks - Contains: - Algae (photosynthetic) - Protozoa (predatory) - Bacteria (beneficial)

How it Works: 1. Mechanical straining - Removes large particles 2. Adsorption - Traps fine particles & some chemicals 3. Biochemical degradation - Microbes break down organics 4. Predation - Protozoa consume bacteria & viruses

Result: Enhanced pathogen removal compared to sand alone

SSF: Advantages & Disadvantages

Comparative Analysis

Advantages: - ✓ Simple, low-cost technology - ✓ Minimal energy required (gravity-fed) - ✓ No chemicals needed - ✓ Sustainable for small communities - ✓ Highly effective (90-99% removal) - ✓ Low operational expertise required

Disadvantages: - ✗ Requires significant land area - ✗ Slow process (24-72 hours) - ✗ Maintenance needed (schmutzdecke scraping) - ✗ Ineffective for high-turbidity water - ✗ Long startup period (2-3 weeks for biofilm)

SSF: Maintenance & Operation

Regular Care Requirements

Schmutzdecke Maintenance: - Scrape top 5-10cm every 2-3 months - Remove accumulated debris - Biological layer regenerates within days

Sand Replacement: - Top sand layer removed during scraping - Replace periodically (every few years) - Bottom sand layers remain intact

Health Benefits Achieved: - Removes bacteria, protozoa, some viruses - Reduces turbidity significantly - Improves taste & odor - Simple monitoring for effectiveness

RSF: Rapid Sand Filtration

Operating Characteristics

Flow Rate: 5-15 mm/hour (much faster than SSF)

Sand Specifications: - Grain size: 0.4-0.7 mm (coarser than SSF) - Depth: 0.5-1.5 meters - Supported by gravel layer - Allows faster water passage

Filtration Duration: 2-8 hours (major advantage)

Pathogen Removal: 95-98% with pre-treatment

RSF: Multi-Stage Process

Pre-Treatment is Essential

Stage 1: Coagulation-Flocculation - Chemical coagulants added (alum, ferric sulfate) - Small particles aggregate into larger flocs - Duration: 15-30 minutes - Essential for water clarity

Stage 2: Sedimentation - Flocs settle under gravity - Large particles removed - Duration: 2-4 hours - Reduces filter loading

Stage 3: Rapid Filtration - Water passes through sand - Mechanical straining - Duration: 2-8 hours - High throughput achieved

RSF: Maintenance Demands

Operational Complexity

Regular Backwashing: - Required every 5-15 days - Reverses water flow through sand - Removes trapped particles - Duration: 10-20 minutes - Energy intensive (requires pumps)

Chemical Management: - Coagulants must be monitored - Dosage adjusted based on water quality - Additional cost for chemicals - Trained operators needed

Troubleshooting: - Filter runs clogged → backwash - Poor water quality → adjust coagulant - Increased turbidity → check pre-treatment

SSF vs RSF: Decision Matrix

When to Use Each

Choose SSF When: - ✓ Low turbidity water source - ✓ Small rural community - ✓ Limited budget/resources - ✓ Minimal operational expertise available - ✓ Land availability not constrained - ✓ Chemical supply unreliable

Choose RSF When: - ✓ High-volume water demand - ✓ Urban municipality - ✓ High-turbidity source - ✓ Trained operators available - ✓ Land area limited - ✓ Chemical supply reliable - ✓ Budget allows operational costs

Case Study: SSF vs RSF Selection

Scenario: Post-Flood Water Treatment

Context: City experiences torrential flooding, water sources highly turbid

SSF Option: - Slow startup (needs 2-3 weeks biofilm) - Cannot handle high turbidity immediately - Suitable ONLY after water clears - Not ideal for emergency

RSF Option: - Coagulants aggregate particles rapidly - Handles high turbidity well - Can operate immediately - Better for emergency response

Answer: Rapid Sand Filter more appropriate for post-flood scenario

Household Level Filtration

Point-of-Use Treatment

Multi-Stage Household Systems: 1. Straining/Pre-filtration - Remove debris, sediment 2. Activated Carbon - Remove chlorine, odor, taste, VOCs 3. Sand Layer - Mechanical filtration 4. Ceramic/Biosand - Fine filtration, pathogen removal 5. Final Storage - Protected container

Activated Carbon Benefits: - Removes chlorine (taste/odor) - Absorbs organic contaminants (VOCs) - Improves water palatability - Does NOT remove microorganisms alone - Requires proper maintenance

Household Filtration Methods

Practical Options

DIY Low-Cost Filters: - Three-Pot Method - Settling for 24-72 hours - Cloth Straining - Reduces turbidity 50% - Biosand Filters - Homemade sand filters - Ceramic Pot Filters - Replaceable cartridges - Candle Filters - Similar to pot filters

Commercial Household Options: - RO Systems - Remove heavy metals, fluoride, nitrates - UV Lamps - Kill microorganisms - Gravity-Fed Filters - No electricity needed - Under-Sink Filters - Integrated with faucet

Household Storage Systems

Rural & Urban Contexts

Rural Household Storage: - Open/covered wells - Earthen pots (cooling effect) - Plastic containers - Overhead concrete tanks - Underground/ground-level tanks

Urban Household Storage: - Overhead water tanks (RCC, plastic) - Covered ground-level tanks - Individual water purifiers with storage - Building-level sump & overhead systems

Critical Point: Covered storage essential to prevent contamination post-treatment

Reverse Osmosis (RO) Systems

Advanced Household Technology

Mechanism: - Semipermeable membrane with pore size: 0.001 micrometers (1 nanometer) - Water forced through under pressure - Removes dissolved salts, heavy metals - Contaminant removal capability: >99%

Effectiveness: - ✓ Removes fluoride, arsenic, lead, nitrates - ✓ Desalination capable - ✓ Improves taste significantly

Limitations: - ✗ Wastewater generation (3:1 ratio - 3L rejected per 1L purified) - ✗ Removes beneficial minerals - ✗ Higher cost - ✗ Requires maintenance & filter replacement

Solar-Powered Water Systems

Renewable Energy Integration

Solar Disinfection (SODIS): - UV-A radiation + heat - Clear PET bottle in sunlight - Minimum exposure: 30 minutes optimal - Effective against bacteria, some viruses - NOT effective against: Cryptosporidium, Ascaris

Solar Pumping: - Photovoltaic panels power pumps - Drives water through RO/UV systems - Sustainable for off-grid areas

Advantages: - ✓ No electricity costs - ✓ Scalable from household to community - ✓ Sustainable technology - ✓ Ideal for rural/remote areas

Limitations: - ✗ Weather dependent - ✗ Lower effectiveness on cloudy days - ✗ May require backup system

Stage 3: Disinfection Methods

Purpose & Types

Primary Goal: Eliminate remaining pathogenic microorganisms

Five Main Methods:

  1. Boiling
  2. Chlorination
  3. UV Radiation
  4. Ozonation
  5. Solar Disinfection (SODIS)

Disinfection Method 1: Boiling

Heat-Based Inactivation

Process: - Rolling boil for 10-20 minutes - All pathogens killed at 100°C - Exception: Clostridium (sporulating organisms) may survive

Advantages: - ✓ Simple, no equipment needed - ✓ Effective against all microorganisms - ✓ No residual chemicals - ✓ Affordable (fuel-dependent) - ✓ Universal acceptance

Disadvantages: - ✗ Energy intensive - ✗ No residual protection (water recontaminates) - ✗ Taste becomes bland - ✗ Not practical for large volumes

Best Use: Individual household, emergencies, bottled water

Disinfection Method 2: Chlorination

Chemical Disinfection - Most Widely Used

Forms of Chlorine: - Bleaching powder - Chlorine tablets/solution - Sodium hypochlorite - High test hypochlorite (HTH)

Typical Dosage: - Residual chlorine: 0.3-0.5 mg/L - Contact time: 30 minutes minimum - Adjusted based on water quality

Advantages: - ✓ Most widely used globally - ✓ Cost-effective - ✓ Provides residual protection - ✓ Effective against bacteria & viruses - ✓ Scalable (household to municipality)

Limitations: - ✗ Does NOT affect: HIV, Polio, Shigella, Salmonella, Cryptosporidium at normal doses - ✗ Forms disinfection by-products (DBPs) - ✗ Taste & odor (chlorine smell) - ✗ Requires careful dosing

Disinfection Method 3: UV Radiation

Light-Based Inactivation

Mechanism: - UV-A/UV-C radiation damages DNA/RNA - Microorganisms cannot reproduce - Kills bacteria & viruses effectively

Advantages: - ✓ No chemicals used - ✓ No harmful by-products - ✓ Effective against most pathogens - ✓ No taste/odor changes - ✓ Rapid disinfection

Disadvantages: - ✗ No residual protection (water recontaminates during distribution) - ✗ Requires electricity - ✗ Ineffective if water turbid (particles block UV) - ✗ Higher initial cost - ✗ Lamp maintenance needed

Best Use: Small communities, household systems with post-treatment storage protection

Disinfection Method 4: Ozonation

Advanced Oxidation

Mechanism: - Ozone (O₃) oxidizes organic & microbial cell membranes - Very powerful disinfectant - Converts back to O₂ (no residue)

Effectiveness: - More effective than chlorination - Works against resistant pathogens - Removes some chemicals/odors

Advantages: - ✓ Powerful disinfection - ✓ No residual toxic by-products - ✓ Improves taste & odor

Disadvantages: - ✗ Expensive technology - ✗ No residual protection - ✗ Ozone is toxic (requires containment) - ✗ Requires skilled operators - ✗ Not suitable for small communities

Best Use: Large urban systems with other technologies

Disinfection Method 5: Solar Disinfection (SODIS)

Household-Level Solar Method

Requirements: - Clear PET plastic bottles - Sunlight exposure - Minimum 30 minutes (longer on cloudy days)

Process: - UV-A radiation + heat (60-65°C) - Kills bacteria & viruses - Some viral inactivation

Effectiveness: - ✓ Bacteria: highly effective - ✓ Viruses: effective - ✗ Protozoa: NOT effective (Cryptosporidium, Ascaris resistant)

Advantages: - ✓ Zero cost - ✓ No chemicals - ✓ Accessible to poorest communities - ✓ Simple to implement

Limitations: - ✗ Weather dependent - ✗ Not effective against all pathogens - ✗ Time-consuming - ✗ Limited to small quantities

Integrated Treatment Approach

Combined Methods for Optimal Safety

Best Practice Sequence:

RAW WATER
    ↓
STORAGE (10-14 days)
    ↓
SAND FILTRATION (SSF or RSF)
    ↓
DISINFECTION (Chlorination or UV)
    ↓
HOUSEHOLD STORAGE (covered)
    ↓
POINT-OF-USE (Optional: RO or boiling)
    ↓
SAFE DRINKING WATER

Rationale: - Redundancy ensures safety - Multiple barriers prevent failures - Fits resources & context

Advanced Water Treatment

For Special Contaminants

Activated Sludge Process: - Biological treatment - Microorganisms decompose organic matter - Reduces BOD by 85-90% - Used in sewage/wastewater treatment - Not typical for drinking water

Constructed Wetlands: - Eco-friendly natural filtration - Plants remove nutrients - Sustainable approach - Lower cost - Longer residence time

Ion Exchange: - Removes hardness - Softens water - Removes some heavy metals - Requires regeneration chemicals

Water Treatment Case Studies

Real-World Application

Case 1: Rural Village (Limited Resources) - Source: Shallow borehole - Challenge: Low turbidity, occasional coliforms - Solution: SSF + chlorination - Cost: Low, sustainable - Maintenance: Local community trained

Case 2: Urban Municipality (High Volume) - Source: River with seasonal variation - Challenge: High turbidity, industrial pollution - Solution: RSF + ozonation + chlorination - Cost: Higher, but serves 100,000+ people - Maintenance: Professional operators

Case 3: Household (Individual) - Source: Municipal supply or well - Challenge: Residual concerns - Solution: RO + boiling + SODIS - Cost: Medium for RO, low for alternatives - Maintenance: Filter replacement as needed

Quality Testing Post-Treatment

Ensuring Safety

Physical Tests: - Turbidity: Should be <1 NTU - Color: Absent or <5 TCU - Taste/Odor: None

Microbiological Tests: - Total coliforms: Absent in 100 mL - E. coli: Absent - Fecal streptococci: Absent

Chemical Tests: - Residual chlorine: 0.3-0.5 mg/L (if chlorinated) - pH: 6.5-8.5 - Key parameters: Based on source water

Monitoring Frequency: - Municipal systems: Daily - Community systems: Monthly - Household: User-dependent
