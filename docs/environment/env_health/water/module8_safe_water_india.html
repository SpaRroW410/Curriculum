Indian Constitution & Water Rights

Legal Foundation for Water Provision

Article 47 - Duty of State: - “The State shall regard the raising of the level of nutrition and the standard of living of its people and the improvement of public health as among its primary duties” - Specifically includes: Clean drinking water provision - Constitutional obligation (not optional) - Enforceable right through courts

Right to Life Under Article 21: - Supreme Court interpretations: Water is part of right to life - Minimum water access is fundamental right - Government must ensure adequate quantity & quality - Discrimination in access illegal

Water Policy Framework: - National Water Policy (1987, updated 2012) - Water Resource Management - Equitable distribution principles - Pollution prevention mandates

Government of India Norms

Official Drinking Water Standards

Supply Norms by Government:

Quantity (per capita per day): - Rural areas: 40 liters per person per day (minimum requirement) - Urban areas: 135-150 liters per person per day - Public institutions: Additional 10-20 liters per person - Industrial/agricultural: Separate allocation

Accessibility Standards: - Rural: Source within 100 meters of habitation - Urban: Tap within household or common yard (>5 households) - Distance: No more than 250 meters for hand pumps (ideal)

Frequency of Supply: - Continuous supply (24-7) preferred - Minimum: Daily supply in fixed hours - Intermittent supply increases contamination risk

Quality Standards: - Must meet IS 10500 (Indian Standards) - WHO guidelines minimum compliance - Zero E. coli standard - Safe for drinking & cooking

Indian Standards IS 10500

Drinking Water Quality Requirements

Physical Requirements: - Turbidity: <1 NTU (or <5 for large systems) - Color: <5 TCU - Taste/Odor: Absent - Temperature: <25°C preferred

Microbiological Requirements: - E. coli: Absent (0/100 mL) - Enterococci: Absent (0/100 mL) - Total coliforms: Absent (0/100 mL) - Heterotrophic bacteria: <10 CFU/mL

Chemical Limits (Essential Parameters): - pH: 6.5-8.5 - Conductivity: <1400 μS/cm - Total dissolved solids (TDS): <500 mg/L - Chlorides: <250 mg/L - Sulfates: <200 mg/L - Fluoride: <1.0 mg/L (or 1.5 mg/L in high-fluoride regions) - Arsenic: <0.01 mg/L (10 μg/L) - Lead: <0.01 mg/L (10 μg/L) - Iron: <0.3 mg/L - Manganese: <0.1 mg/L

Jal Jeevan Mission

National Program for Water Supply

Program Overview: - Launched: August 2019 by Government of India - Goal: 100% Functional Household Tap Connection (FHTC) - Target: Every household gets piped water (Jal Jeevan = Water Life) - Implementation: Ministry of Jal Shakti - Timeline: 2024 completion target

Key Features: 1. Tap water in every house (not communal hand pumps) 2. Adequate quantity (40-55 liters per capita per day) 3. Safe quality (treated, tested regularly) 4. Continuous supply (24-7 target) 5. Community participation

Progress (as of 2024): - ~190 million households covered (out of 300+ million) - ~60% coverage nationally - State variation: Kerala >90%, others lower - Acceleration ongoing

Challenges: - Infrastructure cost (₹3.5 trillion estimated) - Skilled workforce shortage - Maintenance sustainability - Water source availability in drought-prone areas - Quality assurance at scale

Census 2011 Water Access Data

Baseline Assessment

National Water Access Status (2011):

Rural Areas (70% of population): - 94% have access to safe drinking water (improved sources) - Within premises: 46.6% - Near premises: 35.8% - Away from premises: 17.6%

Urban Areas (30% of population): - 91% have access to safe drinking water - Higher within-premises coverage - Better distribution infrastructure

Improved Sources Definition: - Piped water household connection - Public standpipe - Borehole/tube well (protected) - Protected dug-well - Protected spring - Rainwater collection (covered tank)

Unimproved Sources: - Unprotected dug-well - Unprotected spring - Surface water (rivers, ponds, lakes) - Vendor-supplied water carts - Bottled water (quantity limitation)

Census 2011 Analysis

Interpreting the Data

Achievements: - 94% rural + 91% urban = High average (92-93%) - Indicates significant improvement post-independence - Shows commitment to water provision

Hidden Challenges: - “Safe” may not mean actually safe (testing not done) - Within-premises (46.6%) only minority - majority walk for water - 17.6% away from premises - significant burden (fetching time, labor, contamination risk) - Water quality often not verified - Just “improved source” not “safe”

State Variation (Madhya Pradesh example): - Within premises: 23.9% (much lower than national 46.6%) - Away from premises: 30.5% (much higher than national 17.6%) - Indicates rural areas far from source - Accessibility challenge

Limitations of Census Definition: - Does not measure continuous supply - Does not assess water quality - Does not check treatment systems - Does not monitor per capita quantity - Static snapshot (2011 data, updated 2021)

Problem Villages Criteria

Defining Water Crisis Areas

Definition: Villages lacking adequate safe water sources

4 Criteria (ANY ONE qualifies as Problem Village):

1. Distance from Safe Water Source: - No source within 1.6 kilometers - Population must walk >1.6 km to fetch water - Burden on women & children (primary water fetchers) - High labor cost, reduced time for other activities

2. Water Table Depth: - Safe water available at depth >15 meters - Hand pumps cannot reach (require mechanical pumps) - High extraction cost - Seasonal fluctuation risk (dries up in summer)

3. Chemical Contamination: - Water source has excess salinity - Iron contamination (causes staining, taste issues) - Fluoride excess (dental/skeletal fluorosis risk) - Nitrates elevated (cyanosis risk in infants) - Arsenic contamination (cancer risk) - Other toxic elements - Water unsafe despite adequate quantity

4. Disease Risk Exposure: - Water source exposed to risks of cholera or other epidemic diseases - Proximity to sewage/latrine - Flooding risk (contamination) - Livestock grazing upstream - No protective structures - High microbial contamination likelihood

Problem Villages: National Statistics

Extent of the Challenge

Number of Problem Villages: - Exact count varies by state assessment - Estimated: Several thousand to tens of thousands (no single national registry) - Different states use slightly different criteria - Ongoing identification process

State-Wise Distribution (Examples): - Rajasthan: High due to fluoride (western region), depth issues - Andhra Pradesh: Fluoride contamination (coastal areas) - Gujarat: Salinity, fluoride issues - Madhya Pradesh: Mixed challenges (all 4 criteria present in some areas) - Assam: Arsenic contamination - West Bengal: Arsenic (some regions) - Odisha: Distance & seasonal water availability - Bihar: Salinity in eastern region

Population Affected: - Estimated: 100+ million people living in or near problem villages - Vulnerable: Rural poor, agricultural laborers, tribal communities - Impacts: Public health, education (water fetching), livelihoods

Problem Village Case Study

Generalized Scenario Analysis

Village Profile - “Naldih Village”:

Geography: - Semi-arid region, 200 km from nearest city - Population: 8,000 people (~1,600 households) - Occupation: Agriculture (60%), labor (40%) - Income level: Below poverty line (75% of households)

Water Problem Assessment:

1. Distance Issue: - Only hand-dug well in village (1.8 km from center) - Women walk 3.6 km daily (there & back) - 2-3 hours spent on water fetching - During drought: Well runs dry, must travel 8+ km - School attendance drops (children help with water)

2. Depth Issue: - Hand pump installed but at 16m depth - Pump mechanism deteriorates rapidly (maintenance difficult) - Summer: Water table drops to 20m (pump fails) - Seasonal migration occurs - Drinking from unprotected pond during crisis

3. Chemical Contamination: - Well water tests: Fluoride 3.5 mg/L (3.5x safe level) - Previous survey: 47% of children show dental fluorosis - 8 elderly villagers diagnosed with skeletal fluorosis - Health impact: Mottled teeth, mobility issues, quality of life

4. Disease Risk: - Well located 40m upstream of community latrine - During monsoon: Flooding contaminates well - 2020: Diarrhea outbreak (35 cases, 2 deaths in children) - Coliform testing positive (fecal contamination confirmed) - No treatment system in place

Meets ALL 4 Criteria → Official Problem Village

Solution Strategy: Naldih Village

Multi-Intervention Approach

Phase 1: Immediate Relief (Month 1-3)

Issue #1 - Distance: - Install hand pump at village center (0.2 km) - Reduces walking distance from 3.6 km to 0.4 km - Cost: ₹50,000 (~$600 USD) - Community labor for installation - Outcomes: Time saved, school attendance improves, reduced burden

Issue #2 - Depth: - Deepen existing well to tap deeper aquifer (22m) - Motorized pump installed (solar-powered preferred) - Cost: ₹150,000 (~$1,800) - Summer supply ensured - Maintenance training for 3 village mechanics

Phase 2: Quality Improvement (Month 3-6)

Issue #3 - Fluoride: - Install defluorination unit (activated alumina) - Capacity: 5,000 liters/day - Cost: ₹200,000 (~$2,400) - Target: Reduce fluoride from 3.5 to <1.0 mg/L - Training: Operator for maintenance

Issue #4 - Microbiological: - Add chlorination (bleaching powder, 0.5 mg/L) - Daily operator (trained, paid modest stipend) - Cost: ₹30,000/year (~$360) - Regular testing (monthly coliform check) - Community education on boiling during crisis

Phase 3: Sustainability (Month 6+)

Community Management: - Village Water Committee formed (8 members, 50% women) - Water fee: ₹2-5 per household monthly - Revenue: Maintenance fund - Training: Planning, budgeting, operation

Monitoring: - Quarterly water quality testing (government lab) - Annual feasibility study (continued safety) - Health surveillance (fluorosis tracking, disease patterns)

Total Cost & Funding

Investment for Complete Solution

Capital Cost Breakdown: - Hand pump (village center): ₹50,000 - Well deepening & motorized pump: ₹150,000 - Defluorination unit: ₹200,000 - Testing equipment/kits: ₹30,000 - Community building/storage: ₹40,000 - Total Capital: ₹470,000 (~$5,600)

Annual Operating Cost: - Chlorine & chemicals: ₹30,000 - Maintenance & repairs: ₹40,000 - Operator salary: ₹60,000 (half-time) - Testing/monitoring: ₹10,000 - Total Annual: ₹140,000 (~$1,680)

Per Capita Impact (8,000 people): - Capital cost: ₹59 per person (~$0.70) - Annual cost: ₹17.50 per person/year (~$0.21) - Affordable through government programs + community contribution

Funding Sources: - Government: Jal Jeevan Mission (~60%) - Local NGO grant: ~20% - Community contribution: ~20% - Makes solution feasible

Major Chemical Challenges in India

Beyond the Problem Village

Fluoride Crisis: - 66 million at risk in India - Affected regions: Rajasthan, AP, Gujarat, MP, UP, Bihar - Causes: Dental fluorosis (most common), skeletal fluorosis (severe) - Solutions: Well switching, defluorination, dietary changes

Arsenic Contamination: - 10 million at risk - Affected regions: West Bengal, Assam, parts of MP, UP - Causes: Natural geological deposits + industrial contamination - Health impact: Skin lesions, neuropathy, cancer risk (latency 10-20 years) - Solutions: RO systems, well switching, monitoring

Salinity (Brackish Water): - Affects coastal regions, arid areas - Rajasthan, Gujarat, Tamil Nadu impacted - NaCl concentration makes water unpalatable - Causes: Geological, seawater intrusion, over-extraction - Solutions: Reverse osmosis, alternative source development

Nitrates (Agricultural Runoff): - Emerging concern in high-fertilizer areas - Punjab, Haryana, western UP - Cause: Excessive nitrogen fertilizer use - Health risk: Cyanosis in infants (methaemoglobinaemia) - Solutions: Improved agricultural practices, RO treatment

Iron & Manganese: - Common in groundwater - Causes: Natural mineral content - Problems: Staining, taste, odor (not health threat at low levels) - Solutions: Oxidation + filtration, ion exchange

Multi-Challenge Intervention

When Multiple Issues Coexist

Example: Region with Fluoride + Salinity + Distance Issues

Assessment: - Fluoride 2.0 mg/L (needs defluorination) - Salinity: 1,500 mg/L TDS (RO-level treatment needed) - Only source: 5 km away - Population: 10,000

Solution Selection: 1. Centralized treatment plant (not individual home filters) - Economics: Bulk treatment more cost-effective - Capacity: 25,000 liters/day

  1. Multi-stage treatment system:
    • Pre-filtration (sediment removal)
    • Reverse osmosis (removes both fluoride + salinity simultaneously)
    • Post-treatment (pH adjustment, remineralization)
    • Chlorination (residual protection)
  2. Distribution:
    • Piped network to 400 homes
    • Public standpipes (for others)
    • Storage tank (emergency buffer)
  3. Cost:
    • Capital: ₹2,000,000 (~$24,000)
    • Annual operating: ₹300,000 (~$3,600)
    • Per capita: Higher but manages multiple problems
  4. Timeline: 12-18 months implementation

Government Programs & Initiatives

Existing Support Systems

Jal Jeevan Mission (Primary Program): - Direct implementation in problem villages - Provides capital subsidy (60-80%) - Targets: 100% household tap connections - Supports: Water treatment, supply infrastructure

Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana: - Road connectivity + water schemes - Integrated village development - Parallel benefit: Better access

Drinking Water Mission (State-Level): - State implementation of national norms - Water quality assurance - Problem village identification - Capacity building for operators

NGO Partnerships: - UNICEF, OXFAM, local NGOs - Community mobilization - Technical support - Advocacy for vulnerable groups

Challenges to Implementation

Why Problems Persist Despite Programs

Infrastructure Gaps: - ✗ High capital cost (~₹5-10 lakhs for small village) - ✗ Limited government budget allocation - ✗ Coordination issues between departments - ✗ Maintenance systems inadequate

Operational Issues: - ✗ Shortage of trained operators - ✗ Equipment breakdown (spare parts difficult) - ✗ Power supply unreliable (for pumps) - ✗ Water quality testing capacity limited

Sustainability Concerns: - ✗ User fee collection inadequate - ✗ Community management weak - ✗ Leadership changes affect continuity - ✗ Skill gaps in maintenance

Source Depletion: - ✗ Groundwater over-extraction - ✗ Climate change: Increased drought - ✗ Seasonal fluctuation severe - ✗ Aquifer recharge inadequate

Social Barriers: - ✗ Caste discrimination in water access (historically) - ✗ Gender issues in decision-making - ✗ Illiteracy affecting awareness - ✗ Competing resource demands (agriculture vs drinking)

Solutions & Best Practices

Proven Strategies

Decentralized Systems: - Community-managed treatment plants - Shared responsibility model - Local accountability - Sustainable operation

Technology Selection: - Match to local context (not imported solutions) - Cost-appropriate options - Maintenance capacity considered - Climate adaptation included

Community Participation: - Joint planning from start - Training & skill development - Ownership mentality - Equitable decision-making

Government Support: - Sustained funding commitment - Technical backstopping - Capacity building programs - Quality assurance oversight

Innovation: - Solar-powered pumps (renewable energy) - Low-cost filtration (biosand) - Smartphone monitoring (IoT sensors) - Decentralized testing kits

Equity Focus: - Ensure poorest reach included - Gender-sensitive design - Minority community inclusion - Disability accessibility
