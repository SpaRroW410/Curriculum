Purpose of Water Surveillance

Why Continuous Monitoring?

Definition: Systematic monitoring of drinking water to protect public health

Core Objectives: 1. Detect contamination early - Before illness outbreak 2. Verify treatment effectiveness - Ensure purification works 3. Monitor distribution system - Prevent recontamination 4. Track compliance - With drinking water standards 5. Identify emerging threats - New chemicals, antibiotic resistance

Health Impact: - Prevents waterborne disease outbreaks - Protects vulnerable populations - Ensures equitable water access - Provides data for policy decisions

The 11-Point Surveillance Framework

Comprehensive Oversight Activities

WHO & Indian Guidelines Recommend:

  1. Approval of new sources (including private-owned supplies)
  2. Watershed protection - Prevent catchment contamination
  3. Construction & operating procedures approval
  4. Sanitary surveys - Inspect water infrastructure
  5. Monitoring programmes - Regular water quality testing
  6. Certification of operators - Trained personnel
  7. Regulation of chemical substances - Used in water treatment
  8. Cross-connection control - Prevent sewage mixing
  9. Back-flow prevention - Stop reverse contamination
  10. Leak detection control - Identify distribution system failures
  11. Codes of practice - Well construction, pump installation, plumbing standards

Surveillance Activity 1: Source Approval

Evaluating New Water Sources

Assessment Criteria:

Quantity Assessment: - Safe yield adequate for 95% of year - Account for: Peak demand, seasonal variation, population growth - Design period: 10-50 years future projection

Quality Assessment: - Chemical analysis (heavy metals, salts) - Microbiological testing (coliforms, pathogens) - Physical parameters (turbidity, color) - Comparison with WHO standards

Feasibility Evaluation: - Available at reasonable cost - Treatability: Can be rendered safe economically - Accessibility: Proximity to population served - Sustainability: Long-term viability

Approval Decision: - Only sources meeting ALL criteria approved - Private wells require inspection & approval - Regular re-evaluation for continued suitability

Surveillance Activity 2: Watershed Protection

Protecting Source Water Quality

Catchment Area Management: - Area draining into reservoir/water body - Primary protection zone around intake - Prevent human/animal intrusion - Restrict agricultural runoff

Prohibited Activities: - Livestock grazing near source - Human habitation upstream - Waste disposal in catchment - Industrial effluent discharge - Deforestation (increases turbidity)

Implementation Measures: - Boundary marking & fencing - Regular monitoring patrols - Community education - Legal enforcement - Vegetation maintenance

Example: Protected springs require structures preventing direct contamination

Surveillance Activity 3: Water Works Procedures

Infrastructure Standards

Disinfection Protocols: - Plant disinfection after construction - Distribution system flushing - Regular chlorination cycles - Documentation of procedures

Routine Maintenance: - Pipe cleaning schedules - Valve inspection & repair - Reservoir cleaning (annually) - Equipment servicing

Emergency Procedures: - Pipeline break response - Contamination incident protocols - Public notification procedures - Alternative supply arrangements - Boil water advisories (when needed)

Documentation: - Maintenance logs - Test results records - Incident reports - Operator certifications

Surveillance Activity 4: Sanitary Surveys

Field Inspection Assessment

Structural Inspection: - Source protection adequacy - Pipe integrity (no breaks, leaks) - Storage tank condition (cracks, contamination) - Treatment plant operation efficiency - Distribution network assessment

Risk Identification: - Contamination risks (sewage proximity) - Maintenance deficiencies - Operator competence issues - Treatment gaps - Cross-connection hazards

Rating System: - High risk: Immediate intervention needed - Medium risk: Corrective action plan - Low risk: Routine monitoring - No risk: Maintain standards

Follow-up: - Recommendations documented - Timeline for corrections - Re-inspection after fixes - Training for operators if needed

Frequency: - High-risk systems: Quarterly - Medium-risk: Semi-annual - Low-risk: Annual - Emergency situations: Unscheduled

Surveillance Activity 5: Monitoring Programmes

Regular Water Quality Testing

Central Laboratory Services: - Analyze water samples - Equipment maintenance & calibration - Quality assurance protocols - Data management & reporting

Regional Laboratory Network: - Distributed sampling sites - Local analytical capacity - Faster turnaround time - Community access

Sampling Protocol: - Representative sampling (multiple points) - Standardized containers (sterile) - Preservation methods (chilled, chemicals added) - Chain of custody documentation - Aseptic technique (prevents contamination)

Testing Frequency: - High-risk sources: Daily or continuous - Municipal supplies: Weekly - Community systems: Monthly - Small piped systems: Quarterly - Seasonal adjustment for pollution risk

Surveillance Activity 6: Operator Certification

Personnel Competence Assurance

Training Requirements: - Basic water treatment principles - Equipment operation & maintenance - Quality testing procedures - Safety protocols - Record keeping - Emergency response

Certification Levels: - Level 1: Small piped/point systems - Level 2: Large community systems - Level 3: Municipal treatment plants - Level 4: Advanced systems with specialized technology

Educational Pathway: - Classroom theory (fundamentals) - Practical training (hands-on) - On-the-job apprenticeship - Examination & certification - Continuing education (annual/biennial)

Monitoring Operators: - Assessment visits - Performance evaluation - Troubleshooting assistance - Refresher training as needed

Surveillance Activity 7: Chemical Regulation

Treatment Chemical Management

Substances Used: - Coagulants: Alum, ferric sulfate - Disinfectants: Chlorine, ozone - pH adjustment: Lime, soda ash - Corrosion control: Phosphates, pH buffering - Sequestrants: For hardness control

Quality Standards: - Purity specifications for chemicals - Impurity limits (heavy metals, arsenicals) - Certificate of analysis required - Regular supplier audit

Dosing & Monitoring: - Calibrated equipment mandatory - Regular testing of residuals - Documentation of chemical use - Operator training on safe handling

Safety Requirements: - Proper storage facilities - Personal protective equipment - Emergency response procedures - Waste disposal protocols - Staff training

Surveillance Activity 8: Cross-Connection Control

Preventing Backflow Contamination

What is a Cross-Connection? - Unprotected link between potable & non-potable systems - Allows contaminated water to enter drinking supply - Examples: Toilet fill pipes, fire sprinklers, industrial water

Hazards Posed: - Sewage backflow into water supply - Industrial chemicals mixing - Gray water contamination - Pathogenic microorganism introduction

Prevention Measures: - System inspection & mapping - Physical separation (best method) - Air gaps on fixtures - Check valves (one-way only) - Vacuum breakers on hose connections - Backflow prevention assemblies

Regulatory Approach: - Plumbing code requirements - Permits for new connections - Regular audits of high-risk areas - Public education - Penalties for violations

Surveillance Activity 9: Leak Detection

Distribution System Integrity

Why Leaks Matter: - Water loss (volume, cost) - Pressure drop (invites contamination) - Ingress of pathogens into pipes - Distribution system vulnerability

Types of Leaks: - Major leaks: Visible (street flooding, puddles) - Minor leaks: Hidden (slight pressure loss) - Service line leaks: Private property - Main line breaks: System-wide impact

Detection Methods: - Acoustic listening devices - Sound of water escaping - Pressure monitoring - Drop indicates break - Flow monitoring - Night-flow analysis (demand low) - Dye tracing - Follows water path - Thermal imaging - Temperature anomalies - Ground-penetrating radar - Subsurface detection

Repair Protocol: - Isolate affected section - Excavate & repair/replace pipe - Pressure testing before reopening - Chlorine flushing post-repair - Microbiological testing - Documentation & record

Surveillance Activity 10: Well Construction Standards

Codes of Practice for Groundwater

Site Selection: - At least 15 meters from contamination sources - Higher elevation than potential sources - Accessible for maintenance - Community consultation

Sanitary Well Construction: - Proper lining (brick, cement) to ≥6 meters depth - Parapet wall ≥70-75 cm above ground - Cement concrete platform ≥1 meter radius - Drainage slope outward - Covered top (critical for safety) - Hand pump installation with proper foundation

Deep Well Requirements: - Below first impervious layer - Professional drilling required - Strainer properly installed - Water-tight casing - Maintenance schedule

Abandonment Protocol: - Seal derelict wells to prevent contamination - Prevent standing water (mosquito breeding) - Ground-level capping minimum - Proper decommissioning documentation

Surveillance Activity 11: Plumbing Standards

Household Connection Quality

Building Connection Requirements: - Non-corrosive materials (no lead pipes) - Minimum pipe diameter maintained - Proper slope (prevents stagnation) - Isolation valves (for maintenance) - Backflow prevention at connection

In-Home Plumbing: - Separation of drinking water from sewage - No cross-connections to toilets/baths - Proper venting (prevents air locks) - Temperature control (cold water separate) - Regular inspection requirement

Storage Tank Quality: - Sanitary design (sealed, accessible) - Regular cleaning (annually minimum) - Overflow prevention - First-flush exclusion (prevents contamination) - Screening against insects

Inspection & Certification: - Plumbing permits required - Code compliance verification - Pressure testing mandatory - Chlorine flushing post-installation - Microbiological sampling

Sampling Protocols

Aseptic Collection Techniques

Sample Container Requirements: - Sterile containers (critical for microbiology) - Glass or polycarbonate (not metal) - Sealed caps (tamper-proof) - Labeled with date, time, location - Proper volume (100-250 mL typical)

Collection Procedure: 1. Identify location - Fixed monitoring point 2. Flush line - 30 seconds (removes standing water) 3. Flame tap - Optional (kills surface bacteria) 4. Collect aseptically - Avoid touching inside container 5. Seal immediately - Prevent evaporation 6. Record metadata - Time, temperature, observer 7. Preserve sample - Chilled (4°C) or chemical additive

Transport & Storage: - Deliver within 24 hours - Keep chilled during transport - Avoid exposure to light - Document chain of custody - Note any delays or anomalies

What NOT to Do: - ✗ Do NOT rinse container (removes preservative) - ✗ Do NOT collect from just-cleaned taps - ✗ Do NOT collect in dirty containers - ✗ Do NOT leave sample at room temperature - ✗ Do NOT forget labels/documentation

Bacteriological Surveillance

Detecting Microbial Contamination

Presumptive Coliform Test (Traditional): - Multiple tube method with lactose broth media - Gas production in tubes = presumptive positive - Confirms presence of coliforms - Limitation: Not specific to fecal origin

Confirmatory Tests: - EMB Agar: Differentiates E. coli (typical appearance) - Gram Staining: Confirms gram-negative rods - Biochemical Tests: Lactose fermentation, indole production - Culture Isolation: Identifies specific species

Membrane Filtration Technique (Modern): - Water filtered through 0.45 μm membrane - Membrane placed on selective media (mFC, mTEC) - Incubated 24 hours at specific temperature - Colonies counted directly - Advantage: Quantitative, faster, economical

Enzyme Substrate Testing (Advanced): - Presence of enzyme-substrate interaction indicates organism - Rapid results (2-4 hours) - Specific for E. coli - Used in developed countries

Fecal Indicator Organisms

Understanding Test Interpretation

E. coli (Fecal Coliform): - Most specific fecal indicator - Human & animal intestines - Indicates recent/direct contamination - Drinking water standard: Absent (0 CFU/100mL) - If present: System failure, immediate action needed

Fecal Streptococci: - Human & animal origins (distinguishable by species) - More resistant than E. coli - Indicates: Fecal contamination (less recent) - Helps assess contamination age/source - Ratio E. coli:Strep helps determine source

Total Coliforms: - Environmental organisms (some non-fecal) - Include: E. coli + other coliforms - Indicates: Potential for contamination - Drinking water standard: Absent or <5% samples - Less specific than E. coli alone

Clostridium perfringens: - Spore-forming anaerobe - Indicates: Remote/past contamination - More resistant to treatment & disinfection - Used: To assess treatment adequacy - Especially useful for RO/UV system verification

Colony Count Procedures

Heterotrophic Plate Count (HPC)

Purpose: - General indicator of bacterial load - Non-specific (measures total viable bacteria) - Indicates water quality broadly - Early warning system

Procedure: - Dilute water samples (10⁻¹, 10⁻², etc.) - Plate on nutrient agar media - Incubate 48 hours at 35-37°C - Count colonies (30-300 range optimal) - Calculate CFU/mL

Interpretation: - <1000 CFU/mL: Good water quality - 1000-10,000: Acceptable (investigate source) - >10,000: Indicates problem (contamination or stagnation) - Sudden increase: Potential breach in system

Limitations: - Does not identify organisms - Does not distinguish pathogenic from non-pathogenic - Temperature-dependent growth - Some organisms won’t grow on standard media

Chemical Surveillance Testing

Monitoring Contaminant Levels

Physical Parameters: - Turbidity: Nephelometer (NTU) - Color: Spectrophotometer (TCU) - Conductivity: Electrical conductance (μS/cm) - Temperature: Thermometer (°C) - pH: pH meter (0-14 scale)

Inorganic Chemicals: - Heavy metals: Atomic absorption spectrophotometry (AAS) - Ions (chloride, fluoride, nitrate): Ion chromatography - Hardness: EDTA titration - Iron/manganese: Colorimetric methods

Disinfection Residuals: - Chlorine (free & total): DPD method (diethyl-p-phenylenediamine) - Ozone: Specific analyzer - UV dosage: Radiometer measurement

Organic Compounds: - Pesticides: Gas chromatography (GC) - PAH: High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) - Volatile organics: GC-mass spectrometry

Quality Testing Standards

Drinking Water Compliance

WHO Drinking Water Quality Guidelines:

Physical Standards: - Turbidity: <1 NTU (or <5 for systems without continuous monitoring) - Color: <5 TCU - Taste/Odor: Absent

Microbiological Standards: - E. coli: Absent (0/100 mL) - Enterococci: Absent (0/100 mL) - Enterovirus: Absent (0/100 L)

Chemical Standards: - Arsenic: 0.01 mg/L (10 μg/L) - Lead: 0.01 mg/L (10 μg/L) - Fluoride: 1.5 mg/L (upper limit) - Nitrate: 50 mg/L - (See Module 6 for complete chemical list)

Monitoring Frequency: - Microbiological: Minimum weekly (high-risk weekly, small systems monthly) - Chemical: Annual (or more if risk identified) - Physical: Daily visual, periodic testing

Testing Methodology Gaps

Common Challenges

Infrastructure Gaps: - Limited laboratory capacity in remote areas - Expensive equipment maintenance - Shortage of trained technicians - Lack of quality control reagents

Technical Challenges: - Complex matrices interfere with testing - Environmental samples harder than pure cultures - Method validation for local conditions - Equipment calibration drift

Capacity Building Needs: - Training programs inadequate - Continuing education limited - Standardization across regions - Quality assurance protocols weak

Solutions: - Mobile laboratories - Simple field test kits (rapid testing) - Regional central labs (reference standards) - Capacity building partnerships - Digital reporting systems

Rapid Testing Methods

Field-Level Assessment

Advantages of Rapid Tests: - Results within hours (not days) - Lower cost than laboratory testing - Portable (field deployment) - Minimal training needed - Good for screening purposes

Available Rapid Tests: - Colilert system: Most probable number (MPN) in 18-24 hours - Chromogenic substrates: Color change indicates E. coli - ATP bioluminescence: Organic matter indicator - Conductivity probes: Salinity/TDS quick assessment - Turbidity meters: Handheld devices

Limitations: - Less specific than laboratory methods - Cannot identify organisms definitively - Confirmation testing still needed for positives - Variability in technique

Best Practice: - Use rapid tests for screening - Confirm positive results in laboratory - Regular comparison with reference methods

Data Management & Reporting

Surveillance Information System

Data Recording: - Date, time, location of sample - Source identification - Test results with units - Analyst name & signature - Quality assurance notes - Corrective actions taken

Database System: - Digital recording (prevent errors) - Backup systems (prevent loss) - Secure access (confidentiality) - Trend analysis capability - Report generation automated

Reporting Structure: - To water authority (daily for major issues) - To public health department (monthly) - To consumers (transparency) - To government (compliance verification) - International reporting (WHO, SDG tracking)

Public Communication: - Water quality report cards (annual) - Boil water notices (when needed) - Violation reports (transparent) - Community meetings - Website/app access to data

Surveillance Response Protocol

When Contamination is Detected

Immediate Actions (Within Hours): 1. Verify result: Retest to confirm contamination 2. Identify source: Pinpoint location/cause 3. Assess risk: Determine population affected 4. Notify public: Issue boil water notice if needed 5. Alert authorities: Health department, water authority 6. Begin investigation: Trace contamination source

Short-term Response (24-72 hours): - Increase testing frequency - Check nearby samples (pattern analysis) - Inspect infrastructure (pipes, tanks) - Review treatment plant operation - Interview affected persons (symptom surveillance)

Corrective Actions (Days-Weeks): - Repair identified defects - Optimize treatment - Flushing/disinfection of system - Resampling to confirm resolution - Root cause analysis - Prevention measures

Communication: - Initial notice (contamination detected) - Updates on progress - All-clear notice (contamination resolved) - Post-incident review - Lessons learned documentation

Case Study: Outbreak Investigation

Scenario - Cholera Detection

Initial Detection: - 15 cases of acute diarrhea in one area within 48 hours - Stool samples positive for V. cholerae - Public health alert triggered

Surveillance Actions: 1. Immediate testing: Water samples from implicated zone 2. Confirmatory testing: Coliform presence (YES - breach!) 3. Source tracing: Pipeline break near sewage line identified 4. Health response: ORS distribution, IV fluids 5. Communication: Boil water notice issued 6. Community measures: Chlorination, flushing begun

Investigation Findings: - Pipeline break at 7-meter mark - Back-pressure from sewage during low-flow period - Sewage contamination entered supply - ~2000 people affected

Corrective Actions: - Pipeline repaired (24 hours) - System flushing & chlorination (48 hours) - Microbiological testing cleared (72 hours) - All-clear issued - Root cause: Maintenance backlog

Prevention: - Pipeline replacement plan initiated - Increased monitoring frequency - Maintenance schedule intensified - Operator training reinforced
